## Beat 1 — The Hidden-State Problem, In Games

A computer game with hidden information has, structurally, three kinds of state:

- **Public state.** Visible to all players. The board layout. The current round. The score. The tournament bracket.
- **Private-to-one-player state.** A specific player's hand. Their hidden movement plan. Their secret unit composition. Visible only to them.
- **Provably-fair state transitions.** A move from one state to the next has to be verifiable — the dealer can't deal off the bottom of the deck, the player can't claim they had a card they didn't have, the random number used to resolve a battle has to be honest.

On a transparent blockchain, this combination is structurally hostile. Anything you put on-chain is public. So if you want a player to have a hidden hand, their hand can't be on-chain — meaning the game has to lean on off-chain components (a trusted dealer, a centralized server, off-chain randomness) for the hidden parts. At which point you've lost the trustlessness that on-chain was supposed to provide.

People built creative workarounds. Pre-commit-and-reveal schemes for secret moves. Trusted setup for hidden randomness. Multi-sig dealers for poker. Each workaround had its specific brittleness, and **none generalized**.

ZK proofs collapse the problem. State that should be private to one player becomes private state on Aztec, controlled by that player's account. Provably-fair state transitions become ZK proofs that the move was legal *given the hidden state*. Public state remains public. The game design space opens up.

## Beat 2 — Poker, As The Canonical Example

Poker is the textbook example. A player has a hand. The hand is private. They make decisions — bet, fold, raise — based on the hand. The decisions are public. Eventually the hands are revealed.

A trustless, on-chain poker game requires:

- **Each player's hand is genuinely private** until the showdown.
- **Each player's bet is consistent with their hand** (no claiming a hand you don't have).
- **The deal is provably fair** (no one — including a "dealer" — can rig it).
- **The reveal is honest** (no one can change their hand to claim a winning result).

ZK proofs solve all four. A player generates a proof "I have such-and-such hand and I'm betting consistently" without revealing the hand. The deck is generated by a multi-party shuffle protocol with each player contributing entropy, and ZK proofs attest to its validity. At showdown, players reveal the hidden state and a final proof verifies that what they're revealing matches what they committed to earlier.

Poker on Aztec is buildable today. There are demos. The performance is acceptable for casual-stakes games (the prover overhead per move is in the seconds — fine for a turn-based card game).

This pattern generalizes. Anything that's structurally a "card game" — Texas hold'em, blackjack, hidden-role games like Werewolf or Among Us, deck-builders like Hearthstone or Dominion — fits the same shape.

## Beat 3 — Sealed-Bid Auctions And Information-Sensitive Markets

A second canonical case: **auctions**.

In an open-cry auction (eBay, Sotheby's), bids are public. Bidders see each other's offers and respond accordingly. This works for some markets — but it has well-known problems: the "winner's curse" (the winner systematically overpays), strategic shading (bidders bid below their true value, distorting allocation), and runaway escalation.

For these reasons, professional auctions in many domains use **sealed-bid** formats. Each participant submits their bid privately; bids are opened simultaneously; the winner pays either the highest bid (first-price) or the second-highest bid (second-price / Vickrey). Sealed bidding produces better-aligned outcomes when bidders' values are private and uncorrelated.

On a transparent chain, sealed-bid auctions are awkward. You can use a commit-reveal protocol — bidders post hashed commitments first, then reveal in a second phase — but it has known weaknesses: bidders can refuse to reveal (forcing the protocol to penalize non-reveal), the second phase is racy and griefable, and the reveal phase still leaks all bids publicly.

On Aztec, a sealed-bid auction works structurally. Each bid is a private function call: "I bid X, encrypted to my account, with a proof that I have the funds to back it." The auction contract collects the bids privately and runs a ZK-proven aggregation: "the winner is bidder N, the price is Y" with a proof that the answer is correct given the (still-private) bids. Optionally, only the winning bid is revealed; losing bids stay private forever.

Use cases for this aren't just classic auctions. They include:

- **Token launch auctions** (private bids to avoid front-running and price manipulation).
- **NFT primary sales** (avoid sniping and gas wars).
- **Decentralized advertising** (sealed-bid placement auctions).
- **Insurance pricing** (private quote-collection for marketplaces).
- **Protocol parameter negotiation** in on-chain governance (hidden preferences aggregated to a public consensus).

## Beat 4 — Strategy Games And Fog-Of-War

A third category: real-time and turn-based strategy games.

In a strategy game, each player has a private view of the world. Their unit positions, their resource stockpiles, their planned movements — all hidden from opponents. Public state includes the map's permanent features, the round number, eventually-revealed actions like attacks and resource extractions.

This is the classic "fog-of-war" pattern. Every successful strategy game has used it. Translating it to a fully-on-chain, trustless context has been **structurally impossible** on transparent chains.

ZK chains change the picture:

- A player's units are private state.
- Their movements are private function calls that update private state.
- When a player attacks, they produce a ZK proof: "my unit at private location L1 is attacking the opponent's unit at private location L2; I am authorized to attack from L1; the attack resolves under the public combat rules to result R."
- The opponent's loss is reflected in the public state (they lose a unit at L2). The opponent learns: someone attacked at L2. They don't learn: where the attacker is currently positioned (it's still private).

Several Aztec demos along these lines exist. Performance is the limiting factor — generating proofs in seconds is fine for turn-based games but not for fast-twitch real-time. But for the wide universe of games that are *not* fast-twitch — strategy, deduction, simulation, asynchronous multiplayer — the architecture works.

This isn't a side category. **Computer games are a $200B/year industry**, dominated by genres that depend on hidden information. Most of that industry has been outside crypto's reach because crypto's design didn't fit. ZK chains start the bridge.

## Beat 5 — Adjacent Categories Worth Naming

Beyond games and auctions, the "applications requiring hidden state" category includes:

**Sealed-bid procurement.** Government, corporate, or DAO procurement processes that require private offers from vendors. Standard outside crypto, structurally hard inside crypto until now.

**Private negotiation protocols.** Multi-party deal-making where intermediate offers are hidden and only the final agreement is published. Useful for OTC markets, private equity, M&A integrations.

**Confidential prediction markets.** Markets where individual predictions are private but aggregate prices are public. The aggregate is the public good (the market's forecast). The individual prediction is, often, sensitive.

**Hidden-information matching markets.** Job markets, dating apps, organ donor matching, school assignment systems — all rely on participants stating their preferences without revealing them publicly. Most of these run today on centralized matchmakers because trustless decentralized versions weren't possible.

**Private set intersection protocols.** Two parties want to know what they have in common (shared contacts, shared genetic markers, overlapping client lists) without revealing their full sets. Existing cryptographic primitives, ZK-aided, deployable on Aztec.

**Sealed-input scientific competitions.** Submitting models or solutions to a benchmark privately, with a public competition outcome. Useful for ML competitions where revealing your method to other competitors hurts you.

```rust
// A simplified sealed-bid first-price auction on Aztec.

contract SealedBidAuction {
    public auction_open_until: Field;        // public deadline
    public asset_id: Field;                   // what's being auctioned
    public reserve_price: Field;
    public winner: Field;                     // revealed after close
    public winning_price: Field;              // revealed after close
    private bids: PrivateMap<Address, Field>;

    private fn bid(amount: Field) {
        assert(current_time() < auction_open_until);
        // Lock the bidder's funds privately
        lock_funds(context.msg_sender, amount);
        bids.set(context.msg_sender, amount);
    }

    public fn close_auction() {
        assert(current_time() >= auction_open_until);
        // ZK-proven aggregation: find the highest bid
        let (top_bidder, top_amount) = compute_winner_proof(bids);
        assert(top_amount >= reserve_price);
        winner = top_bidder;
        winning_price = top_amount;
        transfer_asset(asset_id, top_bidder);
        // Losing bidders' funds are unlocked privately
        unlock_losing_bids(bids, top_bidder);
    }
}
```

*Annotation: Bids are private throughout the auction. Only at close does the winner and winning price become public. Losing bids remain private forever. The proof generated at close attests "the announced winner had the highest bid in the private set," with no other bid revealed. Compare this to a commit-reveal scheme on Ethereum, which reveals every bid in the second phase and is griefable in the first.*
